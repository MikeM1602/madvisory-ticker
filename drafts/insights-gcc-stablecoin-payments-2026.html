Stablecoins as Payment Instruments in the GCC: Regulation, Licensing and Commercial Reality in 2026

July 2026  ·  10 min read  ·  MENA Advisory

Stablecoins have moved from the periphery of treasury conversations to the centre of payments infrastructure planning. Across the GCC, three separate regulatory frameworks reached advanced or final stages in the first half of 2026, and the window for payments firms to adopt a wait-and-see posture has effectively closed. For CFOs and heads of payments, the question is no longer whether stablecoins will function as licensed payment instruments in the region, but which operational and compliance obligations will apply when they do.

What Is Driving Regulatory Urgency in 2026

Two structural pressures have forced regulators to act on schedule rather than at their own pace. First, the US Genius Act, signed into law in June 2026, created the world's first federal licensing regime for payment stablecoins, defining them explicitly as instruments redeemable one-for-one for fiat currency and used for payment or settlement purposes. That federal clarity immediately raised correspondent banking and interoperability questions for every jurisdiction with active dollar-denominated trade flows, which includes all six GCC states. Second, the European Union's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA), fully applicable from 30 December 2024, had already established a compliance benchmark that GCC-based firms serving European counterparties cannot ignore. MiCA requires issuers of asset-referenced tokens and e-money tokens to hold authorisation from a national competent authority and maintain liquid reserve assets on a 1:1 basis. GCC firms with EU-facing operations are already navigating dual-jurisdiction obligations as a result.

The UAE Framework: CBUAE and VARA in Parallel

The UAE has the most developed stablecoin regulatory architecture in the region and, by most measures, among the most detailed globally. The Central Bank of the UAE published its Payment Token Services Regulation in June 2024, with a compliance deadline that placed licensed activity requirements firmly in force through 2025 and into 2026. Under that regulation, dirham-backed stablecoins, referred to as dirham payment tokens, may be issued and used for domestic payments subject to CBUAE licensing. Foreign currency-backed stablecoins, including dollar-pegged instruments, are permitted for cross-border payments only and face additional reserve and custody requirements.

In parallel, the Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority (VARA) in Dubai published its Virtual Assets and Related Activities Regulations, with stablecoin-specific guidance under the Virtual Asset Issuance Rulebook. VARA's framework applies within the Emirate of Dubai, excluding the DIFC, and requires Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs) to hold a licence covering each activity type: issuance, exchange, transfer, and custody are treated as distinct activities with distinct capital requirements. The minimum financial resource requirement for a VASP conducting exchange or transfer services is AED 700,000 at Category 1 level and rises to AED 15 million at the highest tier. By June 2026, VARA had granted full operational licences to seventeen entities, with a further nine holding provisional approval pending final compliance submissions.

The practical implication for payments firms is that a single product touching both UAE federal payments infrastructure and Dubai-based crypto exchange activity may require two separate regulatory authorisations from two distinct bodies. Compliance teams should map each transaction flow against both frameworks before product launch.

Saudi Arabia and Bahrain: Contrasting Approaches

SAMA has taken a deliberately cautious position. As of mid-2026, Saudi Arabia does not permit the public issuance of private stablecoins for retail payment purposes. SAMA's sandbox has hosted two stablecoin-adjacent pilots since 2024, both involving wholesale interbank settlement between licensed banks rather than consumer-facing instruments. SAMA's published position, reiterated in its 2025 Fintech Strategy update, is that any stablecoin used in the Saudi payments ecosystem must be issued by or in close partnership with a SAMA-licensed entity and must settle in Saudi riyals through the SAMA-operated SARIE real-time gross settlement system. The regulatory stance is coherent and signals that SAR-backed instruments operated by licensed banks are the preferred path, but no formal licensing regime for third-party stablecoin issuers exists yet.

Bahrain presents a different picture. The Central Bank of Bahrain (CBB) updated its Crypto-Asset Module within the CBB Rulebook to introduce a dedicated stablecoin issuer licence category in Q4 2025. The CBB framework requires reserve assets to be held with a CBB-licensed custodian, with daily reconciliation and monthly third-party attestation. Bahrain's positioning as a regional fintech hub means that several firms have used the CBB licence as a GCC entry point, with operational expansion planned into the UAE and Kuwait as mutual recognition discussions progress.

GCC and MENA Context: Remittances, Corridors and Infrastructure Fit

The commercial case for stablecoins in the GCC is inseparable from the region's remittance economics. The India-GCC corridor alone accounts for over $100 billion in annual outflows according to World Bank 2025 data, and the South Asia and Southeast Asia corridors collectively represent the highest-volume low-value payment flows in the world. Average remittance costs on GCC-to-South Asia corridors still exceed 4% in many cases, and stablecoin-based transfer networks have demonstrated settlement times under three minutes at costs below 0.5% in live pilots involving UAE and Bahrain-based operators.

The AFAQ system, which connects RTGS infrastructure across GCC central banks, provides a sovereign settlement layer that regulators are positioning as the ultimate settlement backbone even for stablecoin transactions. The vision articulated by multiple GCC central banks in 2025 and 2026 is a hybrid model: stablecoins handle the retail or SME-facing payment experience, while final settlement between regulated institutions clears through sovereign infrastructure. This architecture reduces systemic risk while preserving the efficiency gains that make stablecoins commercially attractive.

What Payments Firms Should Do Now

The regulatory environment across the GCC is no longer pre-competitive. Licences are being issued, capital requirements are published, and early movers are establishing correspondent relationships and technical integrations that will be difficult to replicate in twelve months. Four actions are immediately material.

First, conduct a jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction licensing gap analysis covering CBUAE, VARA, CBB, and SAMA frameworks against your current and planned product set. Do not assume that a single GCC licence provides passporting rights: no formal mutual recognition framework exists across the six states as of July 2026.

Second, assess reserve and custody obligations under each framework you intend to operate in. The CBB requires daily reconciliation with a locally licensed custodian; VARA imposes specific cold storage ratios for digital asset custody; and CBUAE's Payment Token Services Regulation requires reserves to be held in CBUAE-approved liquid assets with monthly independent verification.

Third, review your AML and Travel Rule obligations. The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) Travel Rule, which requires originator and beneficiary information to accompany virtual asset transfers above USD 1,000, is actively enforced by VARA and the CBB. Firms without a compliant Travel Rule data-sharing solution in production are not in a position to obtain or maintain a licence.

Fourth, engage with the AFAQ integration roadmap. Firms that build stablecoin products isolated from sovereign settlement infrastructure risk regulatory friction as GCC central banks move toward mandating RTGS settlement for stablecoin flows above defined thresholds. Early technical integration with SARIE and its GCC counterparts positions firms ahead of requirements that are likely to become mandatory within the next regulatory cycle.

The stablecoin regulatory moment in the GCC is not approaching. It is already here, and the compliance architecture firms build in the second half of 2026 will define their competitive position for the remainder of the decade.
