BNPL Regulation in the GCC: How SAMA, CBUAE and QCB Are Shaping the Market in 2026

July 2026  ·  10 min read  ·  MENA Advisory

Buy now, pay later has moved from regulatory afterthought to front-line supervisory priority across the Gulf. In the space of eighteen months, all three of the GCC's principal financial regulators have published or enforced operational BNPL frameworks, each reflecting distinct consumer protection philosophies, licensing architectures, and market realities. For payments firms, fintechs, and embedded finance providers active in the region, the question is no longer whether BNPL will be regulated, but how to operate profitably within three divergent rulebooks simultaneously.

Why the GCC Became a BNPL Flashpoint

The structural conditions for rapid BNPL adoption in the Gulf are well documented: a young, digitally fluent population, high smartphone penetration, large unbanked and underbanked segments in certain markets, and a cultural preference for deferred payment that predates fintech by decades. What changed in 2023 and 2024 was the scale. Buy now, pay later volumes across the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar grew sharply enough to draw regulatory attention, as complaints about undisclosed fees, aggressive collection practices, and debt accumulation among lower-income migrant workers reached supervisory desks. Regulators responded not by suppressing the sector, but by building frameworks designed to formalise and contain it.

The timing also reflects a broader regional pattern. Following the implementation of open banking standards, payment token regulations, and instant payment rails, GCC central banks have demonstrably increased their appetite for proactive consumer credit regulation. BNPL represented an obvious next frontier: high growth, limited historical oversight, and direct exposure to retail financial vulnerability.

SAMA: A Licensing Regime Built on Credit Discipline

The Saudi Central Bank, SAMA, moved first and most comprehensively. Its BNPL regulatory framework, issued under the Finance Companies Control Law, requires all providers to hold a dedicated Buy Now Pay Later Finance Company licence before originating any deferred payment product to Saudi consumers. As of mid-2026, SAMA has granted licences to a defined set of operators including Tamara, Tabby, and STC Pay's consumer finance subsidiary, while rejecting or placing on hold applications that did not meet minimum capital and governance thresholds.

The SAMA framework mandates credit bureau integration through the Saudi Credit Bureau, SIMAH, for all transactions above SAR 2,500. Providers must conduct affordability assessments, cap instalment plans at twelve months for unsecured products, and disclose the annual percentage rate equivalent of all charges in a standardised format. Late payment fees are capped, and providers are prohibited from reporting missed payments to SIMAH without first completing a defined notification and cure period. SAMA has also imposed a concentration limit: no single merchant category may account for more than 40% of a licensed provider's total originated volume, a measure designed to reduce systemic exposure to any one retail vertical.

CBUAE: A Dual-Track Approach for a Dual-Market Economy

The Central Bank of the UAE, CBUAE, has taken a structurally different approach, reflecting the UAE's distinct market composition. The UAE hosts both a large domestic retail population and a substantial expatriate workforce that uses BNPL not only for consumer goods but increasingly for rental deposits, medical expenses, and school fees. The CBUAE framework, embedded within the broader Retail Payment Services and Card Schemes Regulation and supplemented by dedicated consumer credit guidance issued in Q4 2024, creates two operational tracks.

Providers offering BNPL products with zero stated interest and four or fewer instalments within a ninety-day window are classified as payment service providers rather than credit providers, and are regulated under the CBUAE's payment services licensing regime. This classification carries lighter capital requirements and faster approval timelines. Providers offering longer tenors, interest-bearing products, or instalment counts above four are classified as licensed finance companies and subject to the full consumer credit regulatory architecture, including mandatory affordability checks, maximum annual percentage rate disclosures, and CBUAE's complaint resolution framework.

Named licensees operating under the payment services track as of July 2026 include Tabby UAE, Postpay, and Cashew Payments. The distinction matters commercially: payment services licensees can onboard merchants directly without a bank partnership, whereas finance company licensees must maintain a dedicated capital buffer and submit quarterly portfolio data to the CBUAE's supervisory division.

QCB: Qatar's Measured Entry

The Qatar Central Bank, QCB, has proceeded with characteristic deliberateness. Its BNPL Services Regulatory Framework, published in November 2024 and carrying a phased implementation deadline of June 2026, is the most conservative of the three. Qatar's BNPL market is smaller in absolute terms, and QCB has used that relative containment as licence to be more prescriptive from the outset, rather than allowing market development to precede regulatory design.

QCB requires all BNPL providers to be either licensed banks, licensed finance companies, or entities holding a newly created Deferred Payment Service Licence. The framework caps individual consumer BNPL exposure at QAR 20,000 across all providers, a cross-provider limit that requires real-time data sharing via QCB's centralised credit registry. Providers must integrate with this registry before activating any consumer account. Marketing restrictions are also more extensive than in Saudi Arabia or the UAE: QCB prohibits BNPL providers from advertising at point of sale using language that obscures the credit nature of the product, and requires all consumer-facing materials to carry a standardised risk disclosure in both Arabic and English.

What Payments Firms Should Do Now

The regulatory divergence across these three frameworks creates both compliance cost and competitive opportunity. Firms that treat GCC BNPL regulation as a single unified regime will make licensing errors. Those that invest in jurisdiction-specific compliance architecture will be positioned to operate where less rigorous competitors cannot.

Four priorities are immediate. First, conduct a product classification audit in each jurisdiction: the UAE's four-instalment threshold, Qatar's cross-provider exposure cap, and Saudi Arabia's tenor limits each carry different implications for product design, and a product that qualifies as a payment service in the UAE may require a finance company licence in Qatar. Second, establish live integrations with SIMAH in Saudi Arabia and QCB's credit registry in Qatar before scaling. These are not optional enhancements. They are preconditions for compliant origination. Third, review merchant agreements to ensure fee disclosure and collection practice clauses align with the SAMA and CBUAE complaint resolution timelines, which differ by approximately fifteen days. Fourth, for firms considering regional expansion, prioritise UAE licensing first: the CBUAE's payment services track offers the fastest path to market and the largest addressable consumer base, with Saudi Arabia as a natural second step given SAMA's more mature but more capital-intensive regime.

The GCC BNPL market will not contract under regulation. It will consolidate around operators with the compliance infrastructure to sustain supervisory scrutiny. Building that infrastructure now, before enforcement cycles intensify, is the defining strategic task for the remainder of 2026.
