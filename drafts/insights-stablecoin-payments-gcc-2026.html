Stablecoins as Payment Instruments: The GCC Regulatory Landscape in 2026

June 2026  ·  10 min read  ·  MENA Advisory

The window for passive observation on stablecoins has closed. With the UAE's Payment Token Services Regulation (PTSR) transition period expiring on 30 June 2026, and Saudi Arabia's central bank accelerating its own digital asset licensing consultations, payment firms operating across the GCC now face a hard compliance deadline and a genuine commercial opportunity simultaneously. The convergence of finalised regulatory frameworks, the first licensed AED-backed stablecoins entering circulation, and the passage of the US GENIUS Act has made 2026 the year in which stablecoins shift from experimental to operational within regional payment architectures.

The UAE Regulatory Framework: What the PTSR Actually Requires

The Central Bank of the UAE (CBUAE) published the Payment Token Services Regulation in mid-2024, establishing the first comprehensive licensing regime for payment stablecoins in the GCC. Under the PTSR, any entity seeking to issue, custody, or facilitate payment using a dirham-referenced payment token must hold a licence from the CBUAE. The regulation distinguishes between Dirham Payment Tokens, which are pegged to the AED and may be used for domestic and cross-border settlement, and Foreign Currency Payment Tokens, including dollar-pegged instruments, which face significantly more restrictive conditions before they can be used in domestic UAE payments.

The transition period granted to firms already operating in the space expires on 30 June 2026. After that date, unlicensed payment token activity is a regulatory breach. Requirements for licensed issuers include full reserve backing with eligible liquid assets, monthly reserve attestations, mandatory custody arrangements with CBUAE-approved custodians, and consumer protection disclosures aligned with the CBUAE's broader retail payment standards. Critically, foreign stablecoin issuers, including large US dollar stablecoins, cannot be used for retail or commercial payments within the UAE unless the CBUAE grants a specific Foreign Currency Payment Token approval. This is not a formality: it represents a structural preference for AED-denominated instruments in domestic commerce.

First Licensed AED-Backed Stablecoins: The Q1 2026 Pipeline

The commercial picture became concrete in the first quarter of 2026 with the CBUAE granting its first in-principle approvals for AED-backed stablecoin issuers. AE Coin, issued by a CBUAE-regulated entity and fully backed by AED-denominated reserves held at licensed UAE banks, received approval to operate under the PTSR framework. A second instrument, issued by a consortium involving a UAE exchange house and a licensed fintech, entered the licensing pipeline. Both are positioned primarily for cross-border B2B settlement and merchant payments, not speculative trading.

For payment firms and corporate treasuries, these approvals signal that the AED-backed stablecoin layer is becoming real infrastructure rather than a regulatory thought experiment. Settlement in AED-referenced tokens, with on-chain finality measured in seconds and reserve transparency auditable in near real time, creates a genuine alternative to correspondent banking for high-frequency, lower-value cross-border flows. The UAE's position as a hub for GCC-South Asia and GCC-Africa corridors makes this particularly significant: the India-UAE corridor alone sees over $18 billion in annual remittance flows, according to World Bank 2025 data.

GCC and MENA Context: Divergent Regulatory Postures

The GCC is not a single regulatory jurisdiction, and payment firms should avoid treating it as one when building stablecoin strategies. The UAE has moved furthest and fastest with the PTSR, creating a clear, if demanding, licensing pathway. Saudi Arabia's Saudi Central Bank (SAMA) has published discussion papers on digital asset payment frameworks but has not yet issued a finalised stablecoin licensing regulation. SAMA's posture in early 2026 remains consultative, with a preference for pilot structures within its regulatory sandbox before any general licensing opens.

Bahrain's Central Bank has an existing crypto-asset module within its Rulebook Volume 6, which provides a licensing basis for certain stablecoin-adjacent activities, but explicit payment stablecoin rules have not been finalised as of mid-2026. Qatar's Qatar Central Bank (QCB) maintains a more restrictive approach, with crypto-asset payments broadly excluded from its payment service licensing framework. For firms building GCC-wide stablecoin payment capability, the practical near-term jurisdiction is the UAE, with Saudi Arabia as the most important forthcoming market to monitor. Strategies should be structured to allow rapid replication into the Saudi framework once SAMA finalises its rules.

The GENIUS Act and Its Cascade Into GCC Corridors

The US GENIUS Act, passed in 2025 and moving into implementation in 2026, establishes a federal licensing framework for US dollar payment stablecoins, requiring full reserve backing, monthly attestations, and primary federal or state supervision depending on issuer size. For GCC payment firms, the GENIUS Act matters because it legitimises USD stablecoins as regulated payment instruments in the world's most important currency. This creates a plausible two-rail structure for cross-border GCC-US and GCC-global flows: AED-backed tokens for inbound and domestic UAE settlement, GENIUS Act-compliant USD stablecoins for international trade flows denominated in dollars.

Treasury and compliance teams should note that GENIUS Act-compliant instruments issued in the US will still require CBUAE Foreign Currency Payment Token approval before they can be used for retail or commercial payments within the UAE. Regulatory arbitrage assumptions, specifically the idea that a US-regulated dollar stablecoin automatically passes muster in Abu Dhabi, are incorrect and potentially costly.

What Payments Firms Should Do Now

The 30 June 2026 PTSR deadline is the immediate forcing function. Firms currently using payment stablecoins in UAE-touching flows, whether for merchant settlements, treasury management, or corridor remittances, must audit their current arrangements against PTSR requirements before that date. Any unlicensed payment token activity after 30 June is a breach, and the CBUAE has stated it will enforce actively.

Beyond the compliance baseline, the strategic actions are as follows. First, assess whether an AED-backed stablecoin layer adds settlement efficiency to your highest-volume corridors, particularly India, Pakistan, the Philippines, and sub-Saharan Africa. The economics of on-chain settlement with licensed instruments are now comparable to, and in some corridors better than, traditional correspondent banking for sub-$100,000 flows. Second, engage SAMA's sandbox process early if Saudi Arabia features in your growth plan. First-mover positioning in the Saudi regulatory conversation will matter when licensing opens. Third, model the two-rail architecture: AED tokens for regional flows, GENIUS Act-compliant USD tokens for dollar-denominated trade, and ensure your compliance framework can handle both reserve attestation regimes simultaneously. Fourth, review counterparty risk in your existing stablecoin exposures. Reserve quality, custody arrangements, and audit frequency are now regulatory minimum standards in the UAE, not optional best practice. Firms holding exposure to unattested or opaquely reserved instruments face both regulatory and financial risk.

The GCC stablecoin landscape in 2026 is characterised by clear UAE rules, an emerging Saudi framework, and a US regulatory anchor that reinforces rather than displaces regional requirements. Payment firms that treat this as a compliance exercise will meet the minimum. Those that treat it as infrastructure will gain genuine competitive ground in the corridors that matter most to the region.
